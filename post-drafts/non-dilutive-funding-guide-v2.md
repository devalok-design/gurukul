## So What is Non-dilutive Funding?

Simple: money you get without giving away ownership of your company. No investors on your cap table, no board seats, no term sheets telling you how to run your business.

This money comes from governments, institutions, universities, and programs that genuinely want to support businesses. They do it through grants (free money), subsidies (they cover a percentage of your costs), interest subventions (they reduce your loan interest), and reimbursement schemes (you spend, they pay you back a portion).

Most founders don't even consider this route. The startup world has made VC fundraising the default narrative, so everyone's optimizing for the next round instead of asking a very basic question: does my business actually need external equity right now?

Sometimes the answer is yes. But a lot of times, especially early on, the answer is no. You just need capital to get things moving, and governments have literally earmarked thousands of crores for exactly that.

The problem has always been that finding and understanding these schemes takes forever. You're reading through policy documents, calling consultants, trying to figure out what even applies to you. That's what AI changes. And that's what this guide is about.


## Before You Start: Three Things I Wish Someone Had Told Me

I ran this research exercise for Devalok (and for one of our clients) and these were the three things that genuinely surprised me:

**You can stack multiple schemes on the same project.** I didn't know this. The government explicitly allows you to combine benefits from different schemes on the same project. So you're not choosing one grant and hoping it covers enough. You're layering Central schemes on top of State schemes, each one covering a different part of your cost structure. The total can add up to more than what you're actually investing.

**Your company has multiple identities in the eyes of the government.** This one changed how I think about applications entirely. Depending on how you write your Detailed Project Report (DPR), the same business can qualify as a "tech startup" for one scheme, an "MSME" for another, a "services enterprise" for a third. Same company, same team, same work. Different framing, different eligibility, different money. This was very true for Devalok because we do design, strategy, AND we're building tech products like Karm and Sarathi. That's three different identities right there.

**The numbers are not small.** On one project we modeled, stacking schemes on a ₹50 Lakh to ₹1 Crore investment mapped to ₹1.4 to 1.9 Crore in potential non-dilutive funding. That's more than the project cost itself. On a larger ₹10-15 Crore capex project, 35-50% of the total cost could come back as grants and subsidies. These aren't token amounts.


## The Method: How to Actually Do This

Alright, here's the part you came for. This is the exact process I used, step by step. You can do this in a single sitting with any AI agent (I used Claude, but this works with ChatGPT, Gemini, whatever you prefer).

### Step 1: Give the AI Everything About Your Business

Don't be vague. The more context you give, the better the output. Here's roughly what I asked:

> "Claude, What are some government schemes for startups or MSME or anything else that Devalok can get non-dilutive funding from (we are not open for giving away equity). You know all about Devalok - please figure out - do a research (both state and central level, we are incorporated out of Lucknow, Uttar Pradesh as Pvt. Ltd. with GST, UDYAM MSME and IEC)"

If your AI doesn't already know about your company, give it the details:

> - Company type: [Pvt. Ltd. / LLP / Proprietorship / Partnership]
> - Incorporation state: [State, Country]
> - Registrations you hold: [GST, UDYAM MSME, DPIIT Startup Recognition, IEC, etc.]
> - Industry/sector: [What you actually do]
> - What you're trying to fund: [Specific project, expansion, equipment, R&D, etc.]
> - Approximate investment range: [How much you need]
>
> "Research both central/federal and state-level schemes. For each scheme, give me: the scheme name, what it provides (grant/subsidy/interest subvention), the maximum amount, eligibility criteria, and application deadlines."

I got back a full breakdown of 8+ schemes with specific subsidy percentages, eligibility criteria, caps, and deadlines. Real policy names, real numbers. Not vague "there might be something" answers.

### Step 2: Ask Which Ones Can Be Combined

This is where it gets interesting. Once you have the list, ask:

> "Which of these schemes can be stacked together on the same project? Are there any that explicitly conflict? Map out the maximum combined funding if we layer all compatible ones."

AI can cross-reference eligibility criteria across 8-10 schemes in seconds. It'll tell you which ones play well together and which ones cancel each other out. A consultant would take days for this. The AI did it in the same conversation.

### Step 3: Explore How to Frame Your Business Differently

This was the part that surprised me the most. Ask:

> "Our company does [X]. But could we frame the same project differently for different schemes? For example, could the same project qualify as [industry A] for one scheme and [industry B] for another? What would the DPR framing need to look like for each?"

Your business is not one thing. A design studio is also a tech company, also an MSME, also potentially a training enterprise. A pet food company is also a food processing unit, also an animal feed manufacturer, also a startup. The label you put on your DPR determines which schemes you're eligible for. AI can map all the overlaps and tell you exactly how to position each application.

### Step 4: Get the Timeline

> "Based on the schemes that apply to us, create an action plan. What do we apply for first? Are there urgent deadlines? What documents and registrations do we need before we can apply?"

This matters more than you'd think. Some schemes have fiscal year cutoffs. Others have limited funds that run out. During our research, we found one scheme (AHIDF) with a deadline literally days away. Without the AI flagging it, we would have missed it entirely.

### Step 5: Take It to Your CA

And this is the important part: AI does not replace your Chartered Accountant or subsidy consultant. Please don't try to file DPRs based purely on AI output.

What AI does is compress weeks of preliminary research into a single session. You walk into your CA's office (or just WhatsApp them, let's be real) already knowing which schemes to target, what the numbers look like, which ones stack, and what deadlines are coming up.

That saves both of you time and money. Your CA's job becomes preparing the actual DPRs and filing applications, not spending the first three meetings explaining what schemes exist.

If you're adept enough, you can even use AI to draft the first version of your DPRs. But get a professional to review them before filing. Framing is everything in these applications.


## Some Schemes Worth Knowing About (India)

I'm listing the ones that came up during our research. This is not exhaustive and your eligibility will depend on your specific business, but it gives you a starting point.

### Central Government Schemes

**Startup India Seed Fund Scheme (SISFS):** Up to ₹20 Lakh as an equity-free grant for prototype/product development, plus up to ₹50 Lakh as convertible debt for commercialization. You need to be DPIIT-recognized and incorporated within 2 years. Administered through approved incubators.

**PMFME (PM Formalisation of Micro Food Processing Enterprises):** 35% credit-linked capital subsidy, capped at ₹10 Lakh per unit. Works through the One District One Product (ODOP) framework. Broader eligibility than the name suggests: "animal feed" is on the eligible list, for example.

**AHIDF (Animal Husbandry Infrastructure Development Fund):** 3% interest subvention for 8 years on loans covering up to 90% of project cost. If your business touches anything related to animal products or feed, this is worth looking into. Total fund size is ₹29,610 Crore.

**National Livestock Mission:** 50% capital subsidy as a direct grant (not credit-linked), up to ₹50 Lakh. This is the closest thing to "someone just hands you money" on this list.

**PMEGP (PM Employment Generation Programme):** 15-35% margin money subsidy for new manufacturing or service units. Projects up to ₹50 Lakh for manufacturing, ₹20 Lakh for service.

### State Schemes (Using Uttar Pradesh as Example)

Every state has its own version of these. I'm using UP because that's where Devalok is incorporated, but the process works for any state. Just swap the state name in your prompt.

**UP Food Processing Industry Policy 2023:** 35% capital subsidy on plant, machinery, and technical civil work. Up to ₹10 Crore in financial assistance. In FY 2024-25 alone, ₹85 Crore was allocated to 70 units. The pipeline is actively moving.

**UP MSME Promotion Policy 2022:** 10-25% capital subsidy on Fixed Capital Investment depending on your region and enterprise size. SC/ST and women entrepreneurs get an additional 2%. Stamp duty exemption and interest subsidies stack on top of this.

**UP Startup Policy 2020:** Prototype grant up to ₹5 Lakh, seed capital and marketing assistance up to ₹7.5 Lakh, patent filing reimbursement up to ₹10 Lakh for international patents. If you have women or transgender co-founders with 26%+ equity, there's an extra 50% boost on seed capital.

Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Telangana, Gujarat all have robust policies with real money behind them. Run the same process for your state.


## This Works Beyond India Too

The method is the same regardless of where you are. Replace "Central and State" with "Federal and State" or "National and Regional" in your prompts and the AI adapts.

**United States:** SBIR/STTR grants for R&D, state economic development grants, SBA programs, USDA grants for rural businesses. Every state has its own economic development agency with funding programs.

**European Union:** Horizon Europe, European Innovation Council, plus country-specific programs like Germany's EXIST, France's BPI France, UK's Innovate UK.

**Southeast Asia:** Singapore's Enterprise Development Grant, Malaysia's MTDC, various ASEAN startup programs.

**Institutional funding:** This one's underrated. Universities (yes, ASU has programs, and I can say that from experience), accelerators, non-profit foundations, and industry associations all have non-dilutive funding. Most people never check because they don't think to look.

The research barrier has always been the reason founders skip this. AI removes that barrier almost entirely. A single session can map out your options in any country.


## Now for the Honest Part

I'd feel weird publishing this without being upfront about the realities.

**Government processes are slow sometimes.** This is true everywhere, not just India. Budget 3-6 months from application to disbursement. Sometimes longer. If you need money next week, this is not your path.

**The paperwork is real.** DPRs, compliance certificates, utilization reports. You will need to stay on top of it. This is not passive income.

**How you frame the application matters more than what you're actually building sometimes.** That sounds cynical but it's just how government schemes work. A good CA or subsidy consultant who knows how to write DPRs is worth every rupee.

**Not everything is a grant.** Some schemes are credit-linked, which means you take a loan first and then get a portion back as subsidy. Some are interest subventions, so you still take the loan but at a reduced rate. Some are reimbursements where you spend first and claim later. Understand the mechanism before you plan your cash flow around it.

**AI can get details wrong.** This is critical. Always verify specific numbers, eligibility criteria, and deadlines against the official government portal or with your CA. Use AI for discovery and mapping. Use primary sources for confirmation. The AI gets you 90% of the way; the last 10% is verification, and that 10% matters.


## Your Checklist

If you've read this far and you're thinking "I should actually do this," here's the sequence:

**This week:**
1. Run the Step 1 prompt with your full business details
2. Get the stacking analysis (Step 2)
3. Explore framing angles (Step 3)
4. Flag any urgent deadlines

**This month:**
5. Get DPIIT Startup Recognition if you don't have it yet (this unlocks multiple schemes)
6. Get Udyam MSME Registration if you don't have it (prerequisite for most state schemes)
7. Sit down with a CA or subsidy consultant, bring your research
8. Prioritize 2-3 schemes to apply for first

**This quarter:**
9. Prepare and file your first DPR
10. Apply to your prioritized schemes
11. Start the next batch

Don't try to apply to everything at once. Pick the ones with the best fit and the nearest deadlines, get those moving, then expand.


## Wrapping Up

Not every business needs a term sheet. Some just need a well-framed DPR and a bit of patience with government timelines.

The capital to support your business might already be sitting in a government portal somewhere. AI just made it a lot easier to find.

Focus on building the business first. The funding to support it will follow.
